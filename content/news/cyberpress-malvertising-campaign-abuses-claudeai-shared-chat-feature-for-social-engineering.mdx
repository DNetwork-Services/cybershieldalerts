A sophisticated malvertising campaign has weaponized Claude.ai&#8217;s shared chat feature to deliver ClickFix social engineering attacks against developers and AI tool users globally, according to research from TrendAI Research. Running from April 8 to June 14, 2026, the campaign deployed 106 unique malicious hostnames across six distinct attack waves in just seven weeks, impersonating at least six major AI brands including Claude AI, ChatGPT Codex, Perplexity, Cursor IDE, and JetBrains. The campaign initially abused GitLab Pages, leveraging the trusted *.gitlab.io domain to host 92 unique malicious subdomains mimicking legitimate software download pages. Malvertising Campaign Abuses Claude.ai Shared Chat Feature Because GitLab&#8217;s free static hosting carries inherent domain trust, it allowed attackers to bypass security filters and target security-conscious users with convincing fake installation pages. Campaign lure themes by impersonated brand or tool (Source: Trend Micro) The critical tactical shift came in Wave 5 (May 6–14), when operators pivoted entirely to abusing claude.ai&#8217;s legitimate shared chat feature. Attackers created weaponized &#8220;shared conversations&#8221; on claude.ai, then purchased Google Ads directing victims straight to these URLs. Since the lure now resided on claude.ai itself, a fully trusted domain with a valid certificate, browser warnings, URL inspection, and Safe Browsing heuristics saw nothing suspicious. By Wave 6 (May 21–June 14), the campaign had completely abandoned self-hosted infrastructure. At least 61 unique shared conversation IDs and 33 new Google Ads campaign IDs were identified in this final phase alone. Victims searching for AI developer tools encountered Google-sponsored ads redirecting them to claude.ai shared chat URLs. These pages impersonated &#8220;Apple Support&#8221; or &#8220;Corda Team,&#8221; presenting polished, step-by-step instructions directing the user to open Terminal and paste a seemingly innocent curl command piped through base64 -d. The decoded payload fetched a remote loader script that first checked for a Russian keyboard layout, a common sandbox evasion and CIS-nation exclusion technique. If no Russian input method was detected, the script downloaded and executed the MacSync infostealer, a macOS-targeting malware that can harvest browser credentials and cookies, SSH keys, and cryptocurrency wallet files before exfiltrating the data to a command-and-control server. Geographic data revealed deliberate regional targeting, with the Asia-Pacific region accounting for 67.2% of all confirmed victim traffic. Taiwan alone accounted for 30.5% of total traffic, with 772 confirmed interactions, far exceeding Japan (201) and Singapore (188). Infection chain (Source: Trend Micro) The campaign&#8217;s later waves showed broadened targeting to include India, France, and Italy, likely reflecting real-time optimization of Google Ads geographic parameters based on earlier performance data. Upon notification from TrendAI Research, Anthropic confirmed it investigated the abuse, banned the responsible accounts, and disabled the malicious shared conversations. The company is also implementing additional abuse mitigations for its shared chat feature. Security teams and developers should avoid clicking search ads for software downloads and navigate directly to official product websites. Any installation process requiring terminal commands copied from a web page, even on trusted domains like claude.ai, should be treated as a red flag. Organizations should deploy endpoint detection for identified payload hashes, educate developers on ClickFix attack patterns, and prioritize official package managers like brew, pip, or npm for tool installation.
