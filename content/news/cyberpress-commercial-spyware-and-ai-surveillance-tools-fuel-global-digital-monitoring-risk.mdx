Governments worldwide are increasingly adopting advanced digital surveillance technologies to monitor individuals, often with little to no legal accountability. A recent threat intelligence assessment by Insikt Group analyzed digital monitoring operations across 193 countries and found that 31 nations pose a high or very high risk to digital privacy. In these high-risk jurisdictions, state actors actively exploit telecommunications infrastructure, commercial spyware, and artificial intelligence (AI) tools to track foreign nationals, business travelers, and political critics. Furthermore, another 74 countries are categorized as medium risk, frequently deploying less sophisticated tracking measures with limited legal oversight. When business professionals or organizations fail to prepare for these monitoring threats properly, they face severe consequences. These dangers include sensitive data breaches, intellectual property theft, targeted espionage, reputational damage, and even physical detention. As commercial surveillance tools become cheaper and easier to acquire, the global threat landscape continues to expand beyond just specialized nation-state intelligence agencies. Spyware AI Surveillance Risks The research identifies five main categories of digital surveillance capabilities utilized by governments. These include network interception, endpoint compromise, platform-level access, public space surveillance, and mass data aggregation. State surveillance risks by country from medium to very high risk based on data collected from 2024 to 2026 (Source: Recorded Future) Network interception involves manipulating internet service providers to monitor web traffic and access stored user communications. Endpoint compromise relies on hacking individual mobile devices or computers using custom malware or commercial spyware. Products like Pegasus and Predator are frequently deployed to secretly extract files, read encrypted messages, and activate a target&#8217;s microphone and camera. Recent investigations have found that commercial spyware is actively used in countries such as Angola, Kazakhstan, and Iraq. Surveillance capabilities by access point and outcome (Source: Recorded Future) The rapid spread of commercial spyware and AI monitoring systems has drastically lowered the barrier to entry for digital espionage. Threat actors and governments can now conduct highly targeted operations to steal corporate secrets or collect massive amounts of personal data. To defend against these growing vulnerabilities, individuals and organizations must adopt strong cybersecurity practices based on the specific risk level of their destination country. According to recordedfuture research, security experts recommend avoiding the use of standard personal or corporate devices altogether. Instead, travelers should carry dedicated, sterile devices that contain absolutely no sensitive data. These temporary phones or laptops should be kept in portable Faraday bags that block electromagnetic signals when not in use. In high-risk and medium-risk environments, individuals must take extra precautions to secure their communications. Travelers should always use virtual private networks (VPNs) when connecting to the internet and rely strictly on end-to-end encrypted messaging applications. Furthermore, devices must have the latest operating system patches installed before travel to block known software vulnerabilities.
