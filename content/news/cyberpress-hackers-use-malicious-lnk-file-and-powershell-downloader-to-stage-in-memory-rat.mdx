Threat actors are executing a highly sophisticated malvertising campaign that leverages Google Ads and trusted developer platforms to deliver data-stealing malware. Disguised as popular AI development tools, this campaign relies on the ClickFix social engineering technique. ClickFix attacks typically trick victims into manually executing malicious commands to fix a fake software error. While many variations use a malicious LNK file and a PowerShell downloader to stage an in-memory remote access trojan (RAT) on Windows systems, this specific operation adapted the tactic to target macOS users via terminal commands. By exploiting the trust technical users place in command-line interfaces, attackers successfully deployed devastating malware directly onto developer machines. The campaign ran from April 8 to June 14, 2026, and rapidly evolved its infrastructure across six distinct waves to evade detection. Initially, attackers used GitLab Pages to host 92 unique malicious domains, bypassing security filters via the trusted *.gitlab.io domain. Early waves also targeted Mac utility users seeking disk cleanup tools, before operators pivoted heavily toward AI developers. When security teams began flagging the GitLab infrastructure, the operators made a dangerous tactical shift. Infection chain of the Claude malvertising campaign (Source: trendmicro) Malicious LNK Stages RAT The operators behind this campaign conducted aggressive keyword testing to identify which AI brands generated the most engagement among potential victims. Over 80% of the campaign traffic specifically targeted users searching for modern AI development environments. The targeting of multiple platforms in rapid succession indicates a highly organized effort to compromise technical professionals. The most alarming escalation occurred when attackers abandoned standalone websites entirely and began abusing Claude&#8217;s shared conversation URLs. By creating weaponized chat sessions on the official claude.ai domain, the threat actors effectively neutralized traditional defensive signals. A breakdown of campaign lure themes by impersonated brand or tool category (Source: trendmicro) Search ads redirected users directly to a real Claude URL featuring a polished, fake support chat from the &#8220;Apple Support&#8221; or &#8220;Corda Team.&#8221; This chat provided step-by-step instructions for the victim to open their system terminal and execute a specific command. The attack sequence relies entirely on user interaction and misplaced trust in the hosting platform. Once a victim clicks the malicious Google Ad, they land on the weaponized chat page. They are prompted to copy an innocuous-looking terminal instruction. This command typically consists of a simple curl request piped through a base64 decoder. When the victim pastes and runs this initial instruction, a secondary script is retrieved and executed, trendmicro said. The malware performs a critical environment check before deploying its primary payload. It specifically examines the system&#8217;s keyboard layout to determine whether Russian input methods are enabled. If a Russian keyboard is detected, the script terminates immediately to avoid infecting systems in the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS). If the system passes this check, the loader script fetches and executes the MacSync infostealer. MacSync is a devastating threat to developers and enterprise environments.
