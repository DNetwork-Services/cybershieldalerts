A long-standing vulnerability in Apple&#8217;s &#8220;Hide My Email&#8221; feature allows almost anyone to uncover the real email address behind a supposedly anonymous alias, and Apple has failed to fix the issue for more than a year despite being warned. The flaw was independently verified by 404 Media using one of its own hidden email addresses, confirming the exploit remains active as of this week. The vulnerability was first reported to Apple in June 2025 by Tyler Murphy, co-founder of privacy company EasyOptOut, who told 404 Media that &#8220;Hide My Email users deserve to know that it may be possible for attackers to discover their hidden email addresses&#8221;. Apple Privacy Feature Fails to Hide Real Email Addresses Murphy said Apple acknowledged in May that it was &#8220;still investigating this issue,&#8221; but no fix has been deployed since the initial report. In limited testing conducted, Murphy found that 100 percent of Hide My Email addresses tested were exploitable, suggesting the bug is not an edge case but a systemic weakness in the service. Hide My Email is a privacy feature bundled with iCloud+ that generates disposable, randomized email aliases so users can sign up for services, apps, and mailing lists without exposing their actual email address. 404 Media stated that messages sent to the alias are automatically forwarded to the user&#8217;s real inbox, and the alias can be deactivated at any time if it starts receiving spam or is compromised. The entire value proposition of the tool rests on the assumption that the alias cannot be reverse-engineered back to the original address an assumption this vulnerability undermines. 404 Media has deliberately withheld the technical mechanics of the exploit, citing the ongoing risk of active exploitation since Apple has not yet shipped a patch. This mirrors standard responsible disclosure practice, where researchers withhold proof-of-concept details for unpatched, high-impact bugs to prevent mass exploitation before a fix is available. The outlet&#8217;s own confirmation test, conducted using a real Hide My Email address earlier this week, ruled out the possibility that the flaw was theoretical or already patched. Because Hide My Email is marketed as a shield against spam, data brokers, and targeted harassment, exposure of the underlying real address could enable stalking, doxxing, or credential-stuffing attacks against users who believed their identity was protected. The prolonged delay of over a year between disclosure and a fix has drawn criticism from security researchers, with Apple providing no public timeline for remediation. Apple has not issued a public statement or CVE identifier acknowledging the flaw, and its only known communication was Murphy&#8217;s account stating that the company was &#8220;still investigating&#8221; as of May 2026. Given the feature&#8217;s integration into iCloud+ and its role in Apple&#8217;s broader privacy marketing, security researchers are urging the company to prioritize a fix and notify affected users once technical details can be safely disclosed.
