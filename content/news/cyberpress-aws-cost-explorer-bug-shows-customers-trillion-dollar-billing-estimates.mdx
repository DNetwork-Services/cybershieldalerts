Amazon Web Services (AWS) confirmed on July 17, 2026, that a defect in its Cost Explorer tool caused some customers to see massively inflated billing projections, with reports showing estimated charges reaching into the trillions of dollars. The issue quickly gained attention across the cloud community, prompting AWS to acknowledge the anomaly and begin an investigation. According to an update posted by AWS Support on X at 2:12 PM, the company is “investigating issues with Cost Explorer reflecting inaccurate estimated billing data.” AWS Cost Explorer Bug Service Update: AWS Billing ConsoleWe are investigating issues with Cost Explorer reflecting inaccurate estimated billing data. Track status: https://t.co/6qSnkuEzc4#AWS #AWSSupport&mdash; AWS Support (@AWSSupport) July 17, 2026 Customers were directed to the AWS Health Dashboard for ongoing status updates as engineers work to identify the root cause. The incident appears to have affected the estimation and forecasting components of Cost Explorer rather than actual billing systems. Users across different regions reported seeing unrealistic cost projections that did not align with their resource usage. In some cases, even accounts with minimal or no active services displayed exaggerated estimates. One user publicly questioned the anomaly, noting that they did not use AWS services at all, yet were presented with alarming cost figures. AWS Support responded by confirming awareness of the issue and reassuring users that the team was actively investigating. Cost Explorer is a widely used AWS-native tool designed to help customers analyze usage patterns, track spending, and forecast future costs across services such as EC2, S3, Lambda, and RDS. A failure in this pipeline, such as incorrect data ingestion, misapplied pricing multipliers, or a calculation error in the forecasting engine, could result in the extreme values reported during this incident. While AWS has not disclosed technical specifics, early indications suggest the issue is limited to the display and estimation layer. There is no evidence to date that actual invoices, billing records, or payment processing systems were affected. However, the scale of the incorrect estimates raised understandable concern among customers, particularly organizations that rely on automated cost-monitoring and alerting systems tied to Cost Explorer data. Cost estimation tools are deeply integrated into financial operations workflows, compliance tracking, and budget enforcement mechanisms. Even temporary inaccuracies can trigger false alarms, disrupt financial planning, and erode user trust in cloud cost management systems. As of now, AWS has not released a detailed root cause analysis or timeline for resolution. The company continues to provide updates through its Health Dashboard while working to restore normal functionality. Further clarification is expected once the issue is fully resolved, including whether the bug originated from a backend service failure or a misconfiguration in the cost calculation logic.
